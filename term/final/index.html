Mental Overload Heatmap

Question: Does mental overload increase later in the day when screentime activity is high?

Data Overview

Data was collected daily from February to March 2026. Each entry includes the number of app switches, notifications received, and self-reported mental overload levels recorded in the morning and at night. Mental overload is measured on a scale from 1 to 5, where 1 represents very low stress and 5 represents very high stress. This allows comparison of how digital activity relates to perceived mental strain throughout the day.

Work Explanation

This visualization shows the relationship between app switching and mental overload at different times of the day. The dataset includes daily information about app switches, notifications, and self-reported mental overload in the morning and at night. Each row represents one day, with two rectangles for comparison: one for the morning and one for the night. The width of each rectangle shows the number of app switches, while the color (from light to dark red) represents the level of mental overload. Darker colors indicate higher overload. This design makes it easy to compare patterns across different days and times of the day. Notification data is represented using the thickness of the rectangle borders, allowing comparison of how interruptions may relate to mental overload.

Observations

  • There is a clear trend where higher app switching activity is associated with higher mental overload, shown by the darker red colors on those days.
  • Mental overload is generally higher at night compared to the morning, suggesting that continuous screen use throughout the day may lead to accumulated cognitive fatigue.
  • Days with lower app switching (such as Feb 13-15) show lighter colors, indicating lower levels of mental overload and less strain.
  • High activity days (such as Feb 23-24) consistently show high overload in both morning and night, suggesting that intense digital interaction can impact mental state throughout the entire day.
  • The variation in stroke thickness shows that higher notification levels often occur alongside higher app switching, which may contribute to increased mental overload due to constant interruptions.
